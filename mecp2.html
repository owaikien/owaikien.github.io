About this project

10th Jan, 2022

Hey! This is a project I done while I was completing my 1-year placement in the London Institute of Medical Sciences. I joined the group of Dr. Peter Sarkies (he moved his lab to Oxford now), which focuses on transgenerational epigenetics inheritance. For those who are interested, I will briefly summarise the background and the results of my work:

Rett syndrome is a rare genetic disorder affecting brain development and causing a decrease in speech and motor functions, cognitive and motor impairment, growth retardation, and seizures. It mainly affects girls and is caused by mutations in the MECP2 gene, which encodes the MeCP2 protein. The MeCP2 protein plays a key role in regulating gene expression in the brain, mainly acting as a transcriptional repressor and being involved in alternative splicing. The mechanism of action of MeCP2 follows the process of DNA methylation, in which MeCP2 binds to methylated cytosine on DNA, affecting transcription. The loss of MeCP2 function is a cause of Rett syndrome and its function is required throughout life for neurological health.

We tested the hypothesis that MeCP2 could affect gene regulation by binding to 5mC in DNA and affecting RNA polymerase activity directly. The results showed that MeCP2 expression, alongside methylation of DNA in the CG sequence context, led to reduced growth in E. coli and associated gene expression changes. These results suggest that MeCP2 can affect transcription directly, potentially by interfering with RNA polymerase progression. However, more research is needed to understand why certain genes are more likely to be affected by direct MeCP2 binding. These findings provide a new angle for understanding how MeCP2 affects transcription in mammalian cells and contribute to our understanding of the loss of MeCP2 activity causing Rett syndrome.
